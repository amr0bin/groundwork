# Accessibility-First Learning Design
### A Self-Paced Path for Early-Career Learning Experience Designers
**Planning Document — v1**

---

## 1. The idea, in one paragraph

This is a self-paced online learning path that teaches early-career learning designers how to solve the problems organizations actually hire them to solve — and does it with accessibility built into the foundation rather than bolted on at the end. The path is organized around the real lifecycle of a learning project, so each stage tackles both a *career* hurdle (getting taken seriously, holding boundaries, proving value) and an *effectiveness* skill (doing the work well). Every module produces a real portfolio artifact, and the whole path produces a complete sample project that demonstrates a designer can run work end to end, accessibly. It is designed to do something most courses don't: stand in for a mentor when no mentor is present, by teaching learners the questions a good supervisor would ask them — so they can ask those questions of themselves, in their own context.

---

## 2. Who this is for

The primary learner is the early-career learning designer: a new instructional/learning experience designer, a teacher transitioning into L&D, a co-op or junior hire, or a career-changer who has the basics but lacks the judgment that usually comes only from being supervised by someone experienced. This is the person you've been informally mentoring and supervising at UBC and TRIUMF. The path is the productized version of that supervision.

A secondary audience worth keeping in view: HR/L&D professionals and accessibility-minded educators who want a structured way to level up. The platform note in section 11 speaks to reaching them.

---

## 3. The four design principles

These are the non-negotiables. They are what make this path distinct from a generic ID course, and every build decision should be checked against them.

**Principle 1 — Accessibility is the thread, not a thread.**
Accessibility is never its own module. Every core skill is taught *through* an accessibility lens by default, using Universal Design for Learning as the legitimizing framework. UDL's premise — that designing for the margins produces better learning for everyone (the curb-cut effect) — gives learners both better craft and a vocabulary they can use with skeptical stakeholders. The pitch to the learner is not "be accessible because it's right"; it's "accessibility is the highest-leverage thing you can be good at early, because it makes your work measurably better *and* sets you apart in a crowded field."

**Principle 2 — Self-coaching replaces presence.**
Because there is no live mentor, the scaffolding has to carry the coaching. The signature move of this path is that every module hands the learner the questions a good supervisor would ask — framed so they can apply them to their own situation. This is the mechanism that lets you "keep making the learning available without having to be there personally." The questions are the product.

**Principle 3 — Portfolio-generating by design.**
Each module produces one concrete, real artifact (a diagnosis memo, a scoped brief, a UDL storyboard, an accessible prototype, an evaluation plan). Finishing the path leaves the learner with a portfolio that walks an employer through a complete project lifecycle. Early-career designers are starved for both portfolio pieces and a sense of how a project actually unfolds; this gives them both.

**Principle 4 — Context-portable.**
The job looks different in higher ed, corporate, nonprofit, public sector, healthcare, and research settings. Rather than teach one sector's version, the path teaches the underlying judgment and then asks the learner to apply it to *their* context through the self-coaching questions. This is why the "ask yourself" framing matters so much: it travels across sectors in a way that prescriptive instructions never could.

## 4. Curriculum architecture: the spine

The path follows the real arc of a learning project. Each stage is a module (or short course). Each pairs an effectiveness skill with the career hurdle that shows up at that stage, and accessibility threads through every one.

| Stage | The effectiveness skill | The career hurdle it addresses | Accessibility thread |
|---|---|---|---|
| **0. Orientation** | How learning design actually works; how to use the self-coaching method | Imposter feelings; not knowing what the role even is | The accessibility-first thesis and why it's an advantage |
| **1. Diagnose** | Is this even a learning problem? Knowledge vs. skill vs. motivation vs. environment | Being handed "build a course" orders with no analysis | Defining *all* learners up front — disability, language, digital access |
| **2. Scope & Negotiate** | Minimum-viable design; scoping under time pressure | Boundary-setting, SME wrangling, being pulled into adjacent work | Resisting the "we'll add accessibility later" trap; building access into scope, budget, and timeline |
| **3. Design** | Structure, sequencing, cognitive load, assessment | Translating expertise into something learnable | UDL *as* the design method — multiple means of representation, engagement, expression |
| **4. Build** | Tool selection; lean production without overbuilding | Tech overwhelm; choosing tools strategically | Born-accessible production; checking as you go, not retrofitting |
| **5. Evaluate & Show Value** | Evaluation frameworks; outcomes and transfer | Proving the work was worth it; demonstrating ROI | Equity lens — are outcomes good *across* learners, not just on average |
| **Capstone** | Assembling the project; writing a designer's statement | Telling your own professional story | Articulating accessibility as your through-line |

A few notes on the spine:

- **Module 0 and Module 1** make a natural free/intro on-ramp (see the platform strategy in section 11). They establish the thesis and deliver an early win.
- The **career hurdles cluster heaviest in Stage 2** — boundary negotiation, time pressure, SME conflict, the "add it later" pressure. That module is where your supervisory experience will be most valuable and most differentiating, so it's worth investing in.
- The **capstone** is where the portfolio becomes a portfolio rather than five loose artifacts. The designer's statement is the learner naming accessibility as their through-line — which is exactly the professional identity the path is trying to grow.

## 5. The module engine (the reusable template)

Every module follows the same internal structure. Consistency is deliberate: a predictable shape reduces cognitive load, which is itself an accessibility principle, and it lets learners focus on the thinking rather than on figuring out the format each time. This template is the heart of the build — get it right once and the rest is content.

**a. Frame the moment.**
A short, realistic scenario of what an early-career designer faces at this stage. Orients the learner and signals "this is about your actual job."

**b. What's really going on.**
The concept/explainer, taught through the accessibility lens by default. Short. Plain language. This is the "skill for effectiveness."

**c. Questions to ask yourself in your context.** *(the signature element)*
The bank of questions a good supervisor would ask. This is what replaces your presence. These are written to be portable across sectors and applied to the learner's real situation. This section does the mentoring.

**d. Worked example + anti-example.**
See it done well, and see it done badly. The anti-example is doing a lot of quiet teaching about pitfalls.

**e. Your move — the portfolio artifact.**
The concrete thing the learner produces. Real, reusable, employer-facing.

**f. Self-review rubric.**
Clear criteria the learner applies to their own artifact — this stands in for mentor feedback. Specific enough that an honest learner can locate their own gaps.

**g. Common pitfalls / how this goes wrong.**
The career-survival layer: the mistakes that get junior designers in trouble, and how to avoid or recover from them.

**h. Reflection + bridge.**
A reflection prompt that consolidates the learning and connects to the next stage of the project.

## 6. Worked example — Module 1: Diagnose

To show the engine running, here is the template filled in for the first content module.

**Frame the moment.** A manager tells you, "We need a course on the new safety protocol by end of quarter." You haven't been asked whether a course is the right answer — you've been handed a solution and told to build it.

**What's really going on.** Not every performance gap is a learning problem. Sometimes the issue is motivation, a broken process, an unusable tool, or an environment that punishes the desired behavior. A designer who builds a course for a non-learning problem wastes everyone's time and quietly damages their own credibility. Diagnosis is the skill of figuring out what kind of gap you're actually looking at — *and* who all the learners really are, before any design decision narrows your options.

**Questions to ask yourself in your context.**
- What change in behavior or outcome would tell us this worked? If I can't name it, do I have a real brief?
- Is the gap knowledge, skill, motivation, environment, or tools? What's my evidence for which one?
- Who are *all* the people who need to succeed here — including people with disabilities, people working in a second language, people with limited bandwidth or older devices, people in different roles or locations?
- Who is being left out of how this is usually framed, and what would change if I designed for them from the start?
- What happens if we do nothing? Is training even the highest-leverage intervention available?
- Who actually owns this problem, and have I talked to them or only to the person who handed me the task?

**Worked example + anti-example.** *(To be produced.)* A strong diagnosis memo that reframes "build a safety course" into a clear problem statement, a defined full-range audience, and a recommendation — versus a memo that simply accepts the order and lists topics.

**Your move — the portfolio artifact: a Diagnosis Memo.** One page. Names the real problem, the full range of learners, the type of gap, the recommended response, and what success would look like. This is a genuinely impressive thing for a junior designer to be able to produce on day one of a project.

**Self-review rubric.** Does the memo name a measurable change, not a topic? Does the audience definition include access and language variation by default? Is there a defensible reason this is (or isn't) a learning problem? Could a stakeholder read it and know what to do next?

**Common pitfalls.** Accepting the solution you were handed; defining the audience as "everyone" (which means no one); skipping diagnosis because of time pressure and paying for it later; treating accessibility as a later question instead of an audience question now.

**Reflection + bridge.** Where in your current or past work were you handed a solution instead of a problem? Next, in *Scope & Negotiate*, we turn this diagnosis into a project you can actually deliver — and into the boundaries that protect it.

## 7. The portfolio outcome

By the end of the path the learner holds a coherent set of artifacts that together tell the story of one complete project:

1. A **Diagnosis Memo** (Module 1)
2. A **Scoped Design Brief** with explicit boundaries and access built into scope (Module 2)
3. A **UDL-aligned storyboard / design document** (Module 3)
4. An **accessible prototype / sample lesson** plus a tool-selection rationale (Module 4)
5. An **evaluation plan** and a one-page value story for stakeholders (Module 5)
6. A **designer's statement** naming accessibility as their through-line (Capstone)

This is the deliverable that makes the path worth paying for: not a certificate, but evidence.

**Open question to resolve (see section 9):** do all learners build these artifacts around a single provided sample project (a "sandbox case"), around their own real project, or either/or? This choice strongly shapes how "in their context" the work feels and how much support material you need to build.

## 8. How accessibility threads through (at a glance)

The point of integration is that accessibility never appears as "Module 6: Accessibility." Instead:

- **Diagnose** — accessibility is an *audience* question: who are all the learners.
- **Scope** — accessibility is a *boundary and budget* question: it goes in the scope, the timeline, and the cost, and you have scripts to defend it.
- **Design** — accessibility *is* the method: UDL.
- **Build** — accessibility is a *production* habit: born-accessible, checked as you go.
- **Evaluate** — accessibility is an *equity* question: outcomes disaggregated, not just averaged.

If a learner finishes and can't separate "good design" from "accessible design" in their head, the integration worked.

## 9. Decisions to make next

These are the forks that most shape the build. None block starting, but they shape everything downstream.

1. **Sandbox case vs. learner's own project vs. both.** The biggest pedagogical fork. A provided case guarantees quality and lowers the barrier for learners with no live project; their own project maximizes context-fit and portfolio value. A hybrid (provide a case, invite those who have a real project to use it) is likely the answer, but it doubles some support material.
2. **Any human touch at all?** The core is self-paced and mentor-free by design. But an optional, asynchronous portfolio review — a paid add-on — could preserve your time while creating a premium tier and a feedback loop. Worth deciding early because it affects positioning.
3. **Credential.** Certificate of completion, a badge, none? Early-career learners often want something to show; decide whether the portfolio *is* the credential or whether you add one.
4. **Scope of "the basics."** How much foundational ID do you teach versus assume? Drawing this line keeps the path tight. A clear prerequisite statement ("you should already know X") may serve better than teaching everything.
5. **Pricing and free tier.** Tied to platform strategy below.
6. **Authenticity commitment.** "Accessibility-first" is a strong public claim. Decide now how you'll involve disabled designers and learners — in examples, in review, in voice — so the path teaches *with* the community, not just *about* it. This protects credibility.

## 10. Build sequence (suggested MVP path)

You don't build seven modules before launching. Suggested order:

- **First, build the engine, not the content.** Lock the module template from section 5 — especially the "questions to ask yourself" bank and the self-review rubric format. This is the reusable asset.
- **Then build Module 0 + Module 1 fully** as a free or low-cost on-ramp. This validates demand, delivers an early portfolio win, and matches the funnel strategy below.
- **Then Stage 2 (Scope & Negotiate),** because it's the most differentiating and the hardest to find elsewhere — it's where your supervisory experience is the moat.
- **Then 3 → 4 → 5 → Capstone,** completing the lifecycle.
- **Pilot quietly** with one or two of the people who already ask you for mentorship, before scaling. They're your first cohort and your best testimonial source.

## 11. Platform note

Your own materials already point to the resolution. Udemy gives you built-in discoverability, fast validation, and an audience of exactly the people you're targeting (aspiring instructional designers, teachers moving into L&D, accessibility-minded educators), but it comes with discounting culture, weak pricing power, and little brand ownership — it's hard to position as premium expertise there. The mentorship-grade value you're building is premium and relational, which Udemy can't hold.

The strategy that fits: put the **intro (Module 0 + 1)** on a discoverable platform like Udemy to prove demand and generate leads, then **funnel learners toward your own ecosystem** for the full portfolio path, where you keep pricing power, brand, and the option of a premium async-review tier. The intro on Udemy is the storefront; the path lives in your house.

---

## 12. Why this holds together

Three goals that could have been three separate products — solve the problems orgs hire designers for, survive the early career, and design accessibly — collapse into one because accessibility is the only competency that pays off on *both* the effectiveness axis and the career axis at once. Teaching it as the foundation, threaded through a real project lifecycle, with self-coaching questions standing in for the mentor and a portfolio artifact at every step, is what turns your individual supervision into something that scales without you in the room.
