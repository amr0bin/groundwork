# Groundwork
### Learning design from the ground up — accessibility built in, not bolted on.
**A self-paced path for early-career learning experience designers**
*From Accessible Learning Labs · Planning Document — v2*

> **What changed in v2:** named the path (*Groundwork*) and its modules; added taglines and the naming logic; and — most importantly — replaced the earlier ad-hoc module template with your own **five-phase rhythm** (Why → See → Try → Share → Reflect), so the planning document and the companion *Module Design Standard* now describe the same module. The medium teaches the method.

---

## 1. The idea, in one paragraph

*Groundwork* is a self-paced online learning path that teaches early-career learning designers how to solve the problems organizations actually hire them to solve — and does it with accessibility built into the foundation rather than added at the end. The path is organized around the real lifecycle of a learning project, so each stage tackles both a *career* hurdle (getting taken seriously, holding boundaries, proving value) and an *effectiveness* skill (doing the work well). Every module produces a real portfolio artifact, and the whole path produces a complete sample project that demonstrates a designer can run work end to end, accessibly. And it is built to do something most courses don't: stand in for a mentor when no mentor is present, by teaching learners the questions a good supervisor would ask — so they can ask those questions of themselves, in their own context.

## 2. Name & positioning

- **Path name:** Groundwork
- **Parent brand:** Accessible Learning Labs. The parent does the distinctiveness work — "groundwork" is common as generic phrasing in instructional-design copy, so it travels best *under* the parent: *Groundwork, from Accessible Learning Labs.*
- **Primary tagline:** Learning design from the ground up — accessibility built in, not bolted on.
- **Alternate taglines:** *The foundation early-career learning designers build everything else on* (audience-forward) · *Build a real portfolio while you learn to ask the right questions* (mechanism-forward).
- **Naming logic (two levels, two jobs):** the *path* carries a building metaphor; the *modules* are named as construction stages (what stage of the project you're in); and your *five-phase rhythm* runs **within** each module (how each module is experienced). Each module name pairs with a plain subtitle so the metaphor never costs clarity — itself an accessibility habit. The system closes on **Capstone**, which is both the academic term and the literal finishing stone of a structure.

## 3. Who this is for

The primary learner is the early-career learning designer: a new instructional/learning experience designer, a teacher transitioning into L&D, a co-op or junior hire, or a career-changer who has the basics but lacks the judgment that usually comes only from being supervised by someone experienced. This is the person you've been informally mentoring and supervising at UBC and TRIUMF. *Groundwork* is the productized version of that supervision.

A secondary audience worth keeping in view: HR/L&D professionals and accessibility-minded educators who want a structured way to level up. The platform note in section 11 speaks to reaching them.

## 4. The four design principles

These are the non-negotiables — what makes *Groundwork* distinct from a generic ID course. Every build decision should be checked against them.

**Principle 1 — Accessibility is the thread, not a thread.**
Accessibility is never its own module. Every core skill is taught *through* an accessibility lens by default, using Universal Design for Learning as the legitimizing framework. UDL's premise — that designing for the margins produces better learning for everyone (the curb-cut effect) — gives learners both better craft and a vocabulary they can use with skeptical stakeholders. The pitch to the learner is not "be accessible because it's right"; it's "accessibility is the highest-leverage thing you can be good at early, because it makes your work measurably better *and* sets you apart in a crowded field."

**Principle 2 — Self-coaching replaces presence.**
Because there is no live mentor, the scaffolding has to carry the coaching. The signature move is that every module hands the learner the questions a good supervisor would ask — framed so they can apply them to their own situation. This is the mechanism that lets you keep the learning available without having to be there personally. The questions are the product.

**Principle 3 — Portfolio-generating by design.**
Each module produces one concrete, real artifact. Finishing the path leaves the learner with a portfolio that walks an employer through a complete project lifecycle. Early-career designers are starved for both portfolio pieces and a sense of how a project actually unfolds; *Groundwork* gives them both.

**Principle 4 — Context-portable.**
The job looks different across higher ed, corporate, nonprofit, public sector, healthcare, and research. Rather than teach one sector's version, the path teaches the underlying judgment and then asks the learner to apply it to *their* context through the self-coaching questions. This is why the "ask yourself" framing matters: it travels across sectors in a way prescriptive instructions never could.

## 5. The spine — modules as building stages

The path follows the real arc of a learning project. Each module pairs an effectiveness skill with the career hurdle that shows up at that stage, and accessibility threads through every one.

| Module | Stage & job | Career hurdle it addresses | Accessibility thread |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bearings** | Orientation — the role, and how to use this path and its self-coaching method | Imposter feelings; not knowing what the role even is | The accessibility-first thesis, and why it's an advantage |
| **Survey** | Diagnose — is this even a learning problem? Who are all the learners? | Being handed "build a course" with no analysis | Defining *all* learners up front — disability, language, digital access |
| **Footprint** | Scope & negotiate — define the boundary and defend it | Boundary-setting, SME wrangling, time pressure | Resisting "we'll add accessibility later"; access in scope, budget, timeline |
| **Blueprint** | Design — structure, sequencing, cognitive load, assessment | Translating expertise into something learnable | UDL *as* the design method |
| **Frame** | Build — make it real without overbuilding; tool selection | Tech overwhelm; choosing tools strategically | Born-accessible production; check as you go, don't retrofit |
| **Proof** | Evaluate & show value — did it work, for whom, and how do you prove it? | Proving the work was worth it; demonstrating ROI | Equity lens — outcomes good *across* learners, not just on average |
| **Capstone** | Assemble the project; name your through-line | Telling your own professional story | Articulating accessibility as your through-line |

Notes on the spine:

- **Bearings + Survey** make a natural free/intro on-ramp (see the platform strategy in section 11): they establish the thesis and deliver an early win.
- The **career hurdles cluster heaviest in Footprint** — boundary negotiation, time pressure, SME conflict, the "add it later" pressure. That module is where your supervisory experience is most valuable and most differentiating, so it's worth investing in. ("Footprint" is the quietly perfect name here: a building's footprint is exactly its scope *and* its boundary.)
- **Capstone** is where the portfolio becomes a portfolio rather than six loose artifacts. The designer's statement is the learner naming accessibility as their through-line — exactly the professional identity the path is trying to grow.

## 6. The module engine: your five-phase rhythm

Every module is built as one pass through **Why → See → Try → Share → Reflect** — the same rhythm from your practice, now used as the build template. This is the reusable unit; lock it once and every module is filling it in. A predictable shape also reduces cognitive load, which is itself an accessibility principle.

- **Why · relevance & activation** — open with the learner's situation and a reason to want this, surfacing what they already know. *This is where the self-coaching questions live* (Principle 2).
- **See · concept-building** — introduce the concept through worked examples and a deliberate anti-example, delivered multi-modally. *This is where UDL is most visible.*
- **Try · active & experiential** — the learner produces the module's **portfolio artifact**, with productive struggle designed in. *Phase 3 is the artifact* (Principle 3), built born-accessible.
- **Share · social learning** — a structured chance to put the artifact in front of another person. *This is the one phase a self-paced, presence-free product structurally threatens — see Decisions, section 9.*
- **Reflect · integration & transfer** — the **self-review rubric** (standing in for mentor feedback) plus a metacognitive prompt that converts a one-off win into portable judgment, and a bridge to the next module.

The full per-phase specification, evidence base, accessibility checks, and the one-page consistency checklist live in the companion **Module Design Standard** — the authoritative build spec. This document and that one now describe the same module.

## 7. Worked example — *Survey* (the Diagnose module)

To show the rhythm running on real content:

**Why.** A manager says, "We need a course on the new safety protocol by end of quarter." You haven't been asked whether a course is the right answer — you've been handed a solution and told to build it. *Questions to ask yourself:* What change in behavior would tell us this worked? Is the gap knowledge, skill, motivation, environment, or tools? Who are *all* the people who need to succeed here — including people with disabilities, working in a second language, on older devices, in different roles? Who actually owns this problem, and have I talked to them?

**See.** Not every performance gap is a learning problem. The concept: distinguishing gap types (knowledge / skill / motivation / environment / tools) and defining the full range of learners before any design narrows the options. Delivered with a strong worked example — a memo that reframes "build a safety course" into a clear problem statement, a defined full-range audience, and a recommendation — set against an anti-example that simply accepts the order and lists topics.

**Try.** The learner produces a **Diagnosis Memo**: one page naming the real problem, the full range of learners, the type of gap, the recommended response, and what success would look like. A genuinely impressive thing for a junior designer to produce on day one of a project.

**Share.** The learner shows the memo to a colleague, SME, or manager in their own setting (the baseline Share approach) and gets a reality check on whether the diagnosis holds — restoring the social phase without requiring your presence.

**Reflect.** *Self-review rubric:* Does the memo name a measurable change, not a topic? Does the audience definition include access and language variation by default? Is there a defensible reason this is (or isn't) a learning problem? *Transferable question:* Where in my current or past work was I handed a solution instead of a problem? *Bridge:* Next, in *Footprint*, we turn this diagnosis into a project you can actually deliver — and into the boundaries that protect it.

## 8. The portfolio outcome

By the end of *Groundwork* the learner holds a coherent set of artifacts that together tell the story of one complete project:

1. A **Diagnosis Memo** — from *Survey*
2. A **Scoped Design Brief** with explicit boundaries and access built into scope — from *Footprint*
3. A **UDL-aligned storyboard / design document** — from *Blueprint*
4. An **accessible prototype / sample lesson** plus a tool-selection rationale — from *Frame*
5. An **evaluation plan** and a one-page value story for stakeholders — from *Proof*
6. A **designer's statement** naming accessibility as their through-line — from *Capstone*

This is the deliverable that makes the path worth paying for: not a certificate, but evidence.

**Open question (see section 9):** do all learners build these artifacts around a single provided sample project (a "sandbox case"), around their own real project, or either/or? This choice strongly shapes how "in their context" the work feels and how much support material you need to build.

## 9. How accessibility threads through (at a glance)

Accessibility never appears as "Module 7: Accessibility." Instead:

- **Survey** — accessibility is an *audience* question: who are all the learners.
- **Footprint** — accessibility is a *boundary and budget* question: it goes in the scope, timeline, and cost, and you have scripts to defend it.
- **Blueprint** — accessibility *is* the method: UDL.
- **Frame** — accessibility is a *production* habit: born-accessible, checked as you go.
- **Proof** — accessibility is an *equity* question: outcomes disaggregated, not just averaged.

If a learner finishes and can't separate "good design" from "accessible design" in their head, the integration worked.

## 10. Decisions to make next

These are the forks that most shape the build. None block starting.

1. **Sandbox case vs. learner's own project vs. both.** The biggest pedagogical fork. A provided case guarantees quality and lowers the barrier for learners with no live project; their own project maximizes context-fit and portfolio value. A hybrid (provide a case, invite those with a real project to use it) is likely the answer, but it doubles some support material.
2. **The Share phase.** The core is self-paced and mentor-free by design, but your method treats social learning as constitutive, not optional. The companion *Module Design Standard* lays out four options; the recommended baseline is *share-into-context prompts* (free, scalable) with an optional *async portfolio-review tier* as a paid upgrade that restores a real Phase 4 without putting you back in the room. **This is the one decision to make early**, because it shapes positioning and pricing.
3. **Credential.** Certificate, badge, or none? Decide whether the portfolio *is* the credential or whether you add one.
4. **Scope of "the basics."** How much foundational ID do you teach vs. assume? A clear prerequisite statement may serve better than teaching everything.
5. **Pricing and free tier.** Tied to the platform strategy below.
6. **Authenticity commitment.** "Accessibility-first" is a strong public claim. Decide now how you'll involve disabled designers and learners — in examples, review, and voice — so the path teaches *with* the community, not just *about* it.

## 11. Build sequence (suggested MVP path)

You don't build seven modules before launching.

- **First, build the engine, not the content.** Lock the five-phase module template and the self-review rubric format (the *Module Design Standard* is that lock). This is the reusable asset.
- **Then build Bearings + Survey fully** as a free or low-cost on-ramp. Validates demand, delivers an early portfolio win, matches the funnel strategy below.
- **Then Footprint**, because it's the most differentiating and hardest to find elsewhere — where your supervisory experience is the moat.
- **Then Blueprint → Frame → Proof → Capstone**, completing the lifecycle.
- **Pilot quietly** with one or two of the people who already ask you for mentorship before scaling. They're your first cohort and your best testimonial source.

## 12. Platform note

Your own materials already point to the resolution. Udemy gives you built-in discoverability, fast validation, and an audience of exactly the people you're targeting, but it comes with discounting culture, weak pricing power, and little brand ownership — it's hard to position as premium expertise there, and the mentorship-grade value you're building is premium and relational.

The strategy that fits: put the **intro (Bearings + Survey)** on a discoverable platform like Udemy to prove demand and generate leads, then **funnel learners toward your own ecosystem** for the full *Groundwork* path, where you keep pricing power, brand, and the option of a premium async-review tier. The intro on Udemy is the storefront; *Groundwork* lives in your house.

---

## 13. Why this holds together

Three goals that could have been three separate products — solve the problems orgs hire designers for, survive the early career, and design accessibly — collapse into one because accessibility is the only competency that pays off on *both* the effectiveness axis and the career axis at once. Teaching it as the foundation, threaded through a real project lifecycle, with self-coaching questions standing in for the mentor and a portfolio artifact at every step, is what turns your individual supervision into something that scales without you in the room. The name says it: it's the ground everything else is built on.
