# Module Design Standard
### A consistency tool built from your own method
**For the accessibility-first path — and reusable for any learning you build**

---

## The core idea

You don't need a new consistency system. You have one. This document turns your **five-phase rhythm** (Why → See → Try → Share → Reflect) and your **three-phase workflow** (Assessment & Design → Development & Testing → Evaluation & Sustainment) into a repeatable standard you can run every module against. Applied reliably, it does two things at once: it keeps everything you build consistent, and it lets early-career designers absorb your method by *moving through it* rather than being told about it. The medium teaches the method.

---

## Three levels of consistency

Drift happens when these get confused. Keeping them distinct is most of the discipline.

- **Course level (the WHAT).** The path teaches your three-phase project workflow and the problems organizations hire designers to solve. This is the subject matter.
- **Module level (the HOW).** Every module is built as one pass through the five-phase rhythm. This is the experience.
- **Artifact level (the PROOF).** Every module produces one real portfolio artifact and one self-review rubric, in a consistent format. This is the evidence the learner walks away with.

A module is "consistent" when all three hold at once.

---

## The module template = your five-phase rhythm

Build every module in this order. Each phase has a job, an evidence basis (yours), an accessibility thread, and a self-coaching prompt that stands in for your presence.

### Phase 1 — Why · Relevance & activation
**Job:** Give the learner a reason to want this before introducing anything new. Surface what they already know and connect it to their real context.
**Evidence:** Self-Determination Theory; prior-knowledge activation.
**Accessibility thread:** "Who are all the learners?" enters as an *audience* question here, before any design narrows the options.
**Self-coaching prompt to the learner:** *Where in my own work have I hit this exact problem, and who was affected that I didn't account for?*
**Build check:** Does the module open with the learner's situation, not your content?

### Phase 2 — See · Concept-building
**Job:** Introduce the concept through demonstrations, worked examples, and a deliberate anti-example. Move from concrete anchors toward abstraction.
**Evidence:** Cognitive Load Theory; Mayer's multimedia principles; UDL.
**Accessibility thread:** This is where UDL is most visible — multiple means of representation, plain language, multi-modal delivery as the default, not the accommodation.
**Build check:** Could a learner with low bandwidth, a screen reader, or a second language get full value from this phase as built?

### Phase 3 — Try · Active & experiential *(this is the portfolio artifact)*
**Job:** The learner produces the real artifact. Productive struggle is designed in, not avoided. Mistakes generate the feedback that teaches.
**Evidence:** Retrieval practice and desirable difficulties; Kolb's experiential cycle.
**Accessibility thread:** The artifact itself must be built accessibly — born-accessible, not retrofitted — so the learner practices the habit, not just the theory.
**Self-coaching prompt:** *What's the smallest version of this I can make real, and where am I tempted to skip the hard part?*
**Build check:** Is there a genuine "make something" here, or did this collapse back into more reading?

### Phase 4 — Share · Social learning *(the phase self-paced threatens — see below)*
**Job:** Consolidate learning in community through discussion, peer feedback, and collaborative sense-making.
**Evidence:** Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development; Wenger's Communities of Practice; social determinants of equitable outcomes.
**Accessibility thread:** Social scaffolding is where equity of *outcome* is won or lost, not just equity of access.
**Build check:** Is there any real other-person interaction, or have I quietly dropped Phase 4? (If dropped, that is a deliberate choice to record, not an accident.)

### Phase 5 — Reflect · Integration & transfer
**Job:** Structured metacognition that converts a one-off win into portable judgment the learner can apply to contexts you couldn't anticipate.
**Evidence:** Metacognition research (Flavell; *How People Learn*); Schön's reflective practitioner.
**Accessibility thread:** Reflection asks "did this work *for whom*?" — the equity lens on the learner's own artifact.
**Self-coaching prompt:** *What would I do differently, and what question will I now carry into my next project?*
**Build check:** Does the learner leave with a transferable question, not just a finished task?

## The Share problem (the one real decision)

Your own evidence base treats social learning as constitutive, not optional. A fully self-paced, presence-free product structurally weakens exactly this phase. You cannot automate it away and stay consistent with your method, so choose one deliberately:

1. **Async community / peer review** — learners exchange artifacts and feedback. Closest to your model; needs moderation or structure to stay alive.
2. **Share-into-context prompts** — the module directs the learner to show their artifact to a colleague, SME, or manager in *their own* setting. No platform overhead; leverages the fact that they're embedded in real organizations.
3. **Premium async review tier** — you (or a trained reviewer) respond to artifacts. Preserves most of your time, creates a revenue tier, restores a real Phase 4.
4. **Deliberate under-service** — accept a weaker Share and over-invest in Reflect to compensate. Legitimate only if named on purpose.

Recommendation: option 2 as the baseline (free, scalable, context-rich), with option 3 as the paid upgrade. That keeps Phase 4 alive without putting you back in the room.

---

## The value rule that protects you under pressure

Why and See are the cheap phases to build (passive content). Try, Share, and Reflect are where transfer actually happens — and they cost more. So the failure mode that destroys *both* consistency and value is identical: a module that is all See and no back half.

**The rule: if you have to cut, cut from See. Never from Try, Share, or Reflect.**

This is the single discipline that keeps your content from drifting toward content-dump under deadline — the exact pressure that erodes everyone else's work.

## The one-page consistency checklist

Run this against every module before it ships. If any answer is "no," the module isn't done.

**Rhythm**
- [ ] Opens with **Why** — the learner's context and reason, not your content
- [ ] **See** delivers the concept multi-modally with a worked example *and* an anti-example
- [ ] **Try** produces a real, born-accessible portfolio artifact with productive struggle in it
- [ ] **Share** has a deliberate, named approach (not silently dropped)
- [ ] **Reflect** ends with a transferable question, not just a completed task

**Accessibility (threaded, never a separate section)**
- [ ] "All learners" defined in Why
- [ ] UDL visible in See
- [ ] Artifact built accessibly in Try
- [ ] Equity-of-outcome lens in Reflect

**Workflow alignment (your three phases)**
- [ ] Evaluation/feedback is planned *before* the module is built, not after
- [ ] There's a decision gate — a point where a learner can course-correct before proceeding
- [ ] The artifact and rubric follow the same consistent format as every other module

**Value guard**
- [ ] The back half (Try/Share/Reflect) carries more weight than the front half (Why/See)
- [ ] Every design choice traces to a reason you could name from your evidence base

---

## Why this maximizes value rather than trading against it

Consistency and value only compete when the structure is arbitrary. Yours isn't — your rhythm is built around the conditions that *produce* durable learning and transfer. So being faithful to the rhythm **is** the value. The checklist above isn't bureaucracy; it's a guarantee that every module does the things your own research says cause learning to stick. Consistency, here, is just your method refusing to be watered down.
