# Module 1 · Bearings
### Orientation: the role, and how to find your footing

*You'll walk away with: your **Starting Map** — a short profile of your context that becomes the lens you point every later question through.*

---

## Why · Let's get oriented

Here's something nobody tells you when you start: the job is strangely undefined. One week you're "the e-learning person," the next you're "the trainer," the next someone forwards you a 70-slide deck and asks you to "make it engaging by Friday." You can do good work for years and still struggle to say, in one sentence, what you actually do.

So let's start there, because the confusion isn't yours to carry — it's built into the field.

You were not hired to make courses. You were hired to change what people can do. Sometimes a course is how that happens. Often it isn't. Holding that distinction is most of what separates a designer who gets pulled in every direction from one who gets trusted with real problems.

*Before you read on, sit with these for a minute — don't rush to answers:*

- When someone asks what you do, what do you say? And does it describe *making things* or *changing things*?
- Think of the last request you got. Were you handed a problem, or a solution someone had already decided on?
- Where do you feel most unsure right now — the craft itself, or holding your ground when people ask for the wrong thing?

*[Choice activity: "What pulled you toward learning design?" — low-stakes, surfaces prior identity. Ungraded.]*

---

## See · What this is, and how it works

**What learning design actually is.** Strip away the tools and the jargon and it's this: figuring out what needs to change, for whom, and designing the most efficient honest path to that change — then checking whether it worked. Content delivery is a small part of it. Judgment is most of it. This path is built to grow the judgment.

**Why accessibility is built into the floor here, not added as a room.** Most programs teach you design first and accessibility later, as a compliance topic, a checklist you bolt on near the end. We don't, for two reasons.

The first is that it makes the work better for everyone. This is the principle behind Universal Design for Learning: when you design for the people at the margins — someone using a screen reader, someone working in their second language, someone on a five-year-old phone, someone who processes differently — you almost always improve the experience for the people in the middle too. It's the curb-cut effect. Curbs were cut for wheelchairs; everyone with a stroller, a suitcase, or a delivery cart benefits every day.

The second reason is more selfish, and I want you to hear it plainly: accessibility is the highest-leverage thing you can be good at early in your career. It is in genuine demand, most of your peers treat it as someone else's job, and being the designer who builds it in from the first conversation makes you visibly different in a crowded room. You'll learn solid foundations either way. Accessibility is the thing that makes those foundations *yours*.

**How this path moves — and a small confession.** Every module here follows the same five-beat rhythm: **Why → See → Try → Share → Reflect.** You start with why it matters in your world, build the concept, make something real, put it in front of another person, and reflect to make it stick.

The confession: you're inside that rhythm right now. This module is itself a worked example of the method. That's deliberate. By the time you've moved through a few of these, the rhythm will start to feel like *your* rhythm — a way you instinctively approach any design problem.

**The real engine: learning to ask yourself the right questions.** I can't sit beside you on every project. So instead of handing you answers, this path hands you the questions a good mentor would ask — the ones you saw above, and a fresh set in every module. Your job isn't to race to the "correct" response. It's to learn to *ask* well, in your own context, because the answers change from sector to sector and job to job, but the questions travel. That skill — turning the questions on yourself before anyone else has to — is the thing you're really building here.

**What you'll have at the end.** Each module produces one real artifact. By the last one, you won't have a certificate so much as a *portfolio* — a set of pieces that walk an employer through a complete project, accessibly designed, start to finish. That's worth more than a badge, though you'll collect a few of those too.

## Try · Make your Starting Map

Before you can ask good questions about your context, you have to name your context. So your first artifact is a short map of where you're standing.

Write half a page — no more — covering:

1. **Setting.** What sector and kind of organization do you work in or want to (higher ed, corporate, nonprofit, public sector, healthcare, research, something else)? What's the culture around learning there?
2. **Your learners.** Who actually has to succeed because of your work? Be specific, and stretch the definition wider than feels natural — roles, prior knowledge, languages, access needs, the devices and conditions they're really using.
3. **What lands on your desk.** What kinds of requests do you get? Problems, or pre-decided solutions?
4. **Your shaky ground.** Where do you feel least sure right now? Name it honestly; it's where the most growth is hiding.

Keep this. In every module that follows, when you hit a set of self-coaching questions, you'll point them at *this* context. The Starting Map is your lens.

*[Assignment activity, online text or file upload. This is portfolio piece #1.]*

---

## Share · Say hello, out loud

Two options, pick what fits:

- Post one line from your Starting Map in the group space — your setting and your shakiest ground. Read two others. You'll find you're not the only one. *[Forum]*
- Or, offline: tell a colleague the sentence you'd now use to describe what you do — "I'm hired to change what people can do, not to make courses" — and see how they react. Notice whether it changes the conversation.

---

## Reflect · Before you move on

*Check your Starting Map against these:*

- Did I name *specific* people and conditions, or did I write "all learners" and move on? (If you wrote "everyone," you've named no one — go back.)
- Is my shaky ground honest, or did I write something safe?

*And one question to carry forward:*

> What do I want to be true about how I work that isn't quite true yet?

You now have your bearings: a sense of the role, the method, and your own ground. Next, in **Survey**, we walk onto a real site and learn the first move of any project — figuring out whether there's even a learning problem here at all, and who all the people are that we're designing for.

# Module 2 · Survey
### Diagnose: is this even a learning problem? And who are all the learners?

*You'll walk away with: a one-page **Diagnosis Memo** — the document that turns a vague order into a real, designable problem.*

---

## Why · The order that isn't a brief

Picture it. A manager catches you in the hall: *"We need a course on the new safety protocol. Can you have something by end of quarter?"*

Notice what just happened. You weren't asked whether a course is the right answer. You were handed a solution — "a course" — and a deadline, and the actual problem was never named. This is the single most common way early-career designers get set up to fail: you build exactly what you were told, it doesn't move the needle, and somehow that lands on you.

The move that protects you — and your credibility — is to slow down for one short, decisive step before anything else: diagnosis. Not a six-week study. A focused look at what's really going on.

*Point these at the situation you're actually in. Use your Starting Map:*

- If this training works perfectly, what will people be *doing* differently? If I can't answer that, do I even have a brief yet?
- Is the gap really about *not knowing how* — or is it about motivation, a broken process, a missing tool, or an environment that punishes the right behavior?
- Who are **all** the people who need to succeed here? Not the average user — the full range, including the ones usually left out.
- Who actually owns this problem, and have I talked to them, or only to the person who handed me the task?

---

## See · How to tell what you're really looking at

**Not every gap is a learning problem.** This is the most useful idea in the module, so here it is directly: training only fixes problems caused by people *not knowing how*. If the real issue is something else, a course won't touch it — and building one anyway wastes everyone's time, including your reputation's.

There's a classic test for this, from Mager and Pipe's work on analyzing performance problems. Ask: *if their life depended on it, could they do it right now?* If yes — they could, if sufficiently motivated — then it isn't a skill-or-knowledge gap, and training is the wrong tool. You're looking at one of the other gap types instead:

- **Knowledge** — they don't have the information. *(Training can help.)*
- **Skill** — they have the information but can't yet do it. *(Training can help.)*
- **Motivation** — they can do it but don't. *(Training won't fix this. Incentives, feedback, or leadership will.)*
- **Environment** — something in the system blocks them. *(Fix the system.)*
- **Tools** — the equipment or interface fails them. *(Fix the tool.)*

A designer who can name which of these is in play — and say so out loud — is worth ten who just start building.

**"Who are all the learners?" is where accessibility begins.** Here's the quiet thing about diagnosis: the moment you define your audience, you've already made your biggest accessibility decision, whether you noticed or not. If you picture a "typical" learner — sighted, fluent, focused, on a good laptop, with time — you've designed everyone else out before you've made a single slide. So we define the full range *now*, at the very start, when it's free to do. Who has a disability you should design for? Who's working in a second language? Who's on a shared device, a small screen, a noisy floor, a night shift? This isn't a separate accessibility step. It's just doing diagnosis honestly.

**What good looks like — and what doesn't.**

*A strong diagnosis (the reframe):*

> **Request as given:** "A course on the new safety protocol."
> **What's really going on:** Incidents rose after the protocol changed. But staff describe the new procedure as unclear and the sign-off form as confusing — and the old habit is still faster. This looks like a mix of *knowledge* (the change wasn't taught) and *environment* (the form fights the behavior). Training alone won't fix the environment part.
> **All the learners:** Floor staff across three shifts, including night staff who missed the rollout meeting; two team leads who are deaf and rely on captions; several staff whose first language isn't English; everyone accessing this on shared shop-floor tablets.
> **Recommended response:** A short, accessible learning piece on the *what and why* of the change — paired with a flag to operations that the sign-off form needs simplifying. Training the part that's a learning problem; naming the part that isn't.
> **Success looks like:** Incidents down next quarter; staff can state the change in their own words; the form gets fixed.

*A weak one (the trap):*

> "Safety Protocol Course. Module 1: Introduction. Module 2: The Old Procedure. Module 3: The New Procedure. Module 4: Quiz. Audience: all staff."

The second one accepted the order, never asked what was wrong, flattened everyone into "all staff," and guaranteed a course that ticks a box and changes nothing. The difference between the two isn't talent. It's that the first one *paused to diagnose.*

## Try · Write your Diagnosis Memo

Take a real request — one from your own work if you have it, or the safety-protocol scenario if you don't — and write a one-page memo with these five parts:

1. **The request as given.** Quote it, solution and all.
2. **What's really going on.** Name the gap type(s) and your evidence. Be willing to say "part of this isn't a learning problem."
3. **All the learners.** The full range, specifically — including access, language, device, and shift/role variation.
4. **Recommended response.** What you'd actually do, including what you'd *hand to someone else* because it isn't yours to fix with training.
5. **What success looks like.** A change you could actually observe.

One page. The discipline of fitting it on one page is part of the skill.

*[Assignment activity. Portfolio piece #2. Attach the rubric below as a Moodle Rubric so learners can self-apply it.]*

---

## Share · Get a reality check

Diagnosis is a claim about reality, so test it against someone who knows the reality:

- **In your setting:** show your memo to a colleague, an SME, or the person who made the original request. Ask one question — "Does this match what you're seeing?" — and listen. Watch especially for where they push back; that's information.
- **Or in the group:** post your reframe — the "request as given" versus "what's really going on." Read two others and tell each person one thing their diagnosis made you notice. *[Forum or, when enough learners are active, the Workshop activity for structured peer review against the rubric.]*

---

## Reflect · Before you move on

*Hold your memo against this rubric — the same one a mentor would use:*

| | Strong | Needs another pass |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem, not topic** | Names a change people would *do* | Lists subjects to cover |
| **Honest gap analysis** | Says which gaps aren't learning problems | Assumes training is the answer |
| **All the learners** | Specific range incl. access & language | "All staff" / "everyone" |
| **Defensible** | A stakeholder could read it and act | Vague or unfalsifiable |
| **Right-sized** | Fits on a page | Sprawls, or says nothing |

*And one question to carry forward:*

> Where, in my current or past work, was I handed a solution when I should have been handed a problem — and what would I do differently now?

That's the first move of any project: refusing to build until you know what you're building and who you're building it for. Next, in **Footprint**, we take this diagnosis and turn it into a project you can actually deliver — drawing the boundary around the work, defending it under deadline pressure, and making sure accessibility lives *inside* the scope instead of getting promised for "later."
